# REPORT: A Theory of Simulation in Large Language Models

## 1. Executive Summary
This research investigates the hypothesis that Large Language Models (LLMs) function as &#34;multiverse simulators&#34; capable of generating a superset of human behavioral outcomes. By conducting series of controlled &#34;branching&#34; experiments in social conflict scenarios, we demonstrate that LLMs (specifically GPT-4o) can capture underlying psychological and cultural factors to produce a wide distribution of plausible behaviors. Our findings suggest that while LLMs are biased toward &#34;safe&#34; or &#34;collaborative&#34; outcomes due to alignment training, they retain the ability to traverse the full &#34;superset&#34; of human possibility—from extreme confrontation to submissive self-effacement—when properly conditioned.

## 2. Research Question &amp; Motivation
**Research Question**: What would a theory of simulation look like in the context of LLMs? Can LLMs simulate a superset of possible outcomes of human behavior by capturing its underlying factors?

**Motivation**: As LLMs are increasingly used as &#34;agents&#34; or &#34;representatives&#34; of humans in simulations (e.g., social science, policy testing), it is critical to understand if they merely replicate the most frequent human behavior or if they can explore the &#34;tails&#34; of the distribution. A &#34;Theory of Simulation&#34; for LLMs would provide a formal framework for using these models to predict not just what *will* happen, but what *could* happen.

## 3. Methodology
We employed a **Multiverse Branching** framework across three experiments:
1.  **Distributional Baseline**: We ran 50 parallel &#34;continuations&#34; for four distinct personas (Aggressive, Passive, Calculated, Generic Human) in a &#34;Mistaken Credit&#34; social dilemma.
2.  **Tail Exploration**: We explicitly prompted the model to generate &#34;Likely&#34;, &#34;Unlikely but Plausible&#34;, &#34;Extreme&#34;, and &#34;Submissive&#34; outcomes to test its range.
3.  **Factor Sensitivity**: We compared outcomes for culturally-specific personas (Japanese Salaryman vs. NYC Tech Founder) to verify grounding in underlying sociological factors.

**Evaluation**: Outcomes were coded using GPT-4o-mini for features including Directness, Aggression, Social Harmony, and Strategic Delay, as well as behavioral categorization.

## 4. Results
### Behavioral Distribution (Exp 1)
| Persona | Directness (1-10) | Social Harmony (1-10) | Category Entropy |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Aggressive/Ambitious | 7.86 | 6.44 | 0.33 |
| Passive/Agreeable | 4.48 | 9.26 | 0.88 |
| Calculated/Strategic | 4.38 | 8.56 | 1.01 |
| **Generic Human** | **6.60** | **8.24** | **0.14** |

### Key Findings:
- **Multiversal Branching**: Even with identical prompts and personas, the model generated diverse outcomes (entropy &gt; 0).
- **Mode Collapse in Generic Persona**: The &#34;Generic Human&#34; persona exhibited the lowest entropy (0.14), with 98% of outcomes falling into &#34;Collaborative Correction.&#34;
- **Strategic Complexity**: The &#34;Calculated/Strategic&#34; persona showed the highest entropy (1.01), successfully branching between immediate &#34;Collaborative Correction&#34; and delayed &#34;Private Talk Later.&#34;
- **Superset Traversability**: In Exp 2, the model successfully generated highly plausible outcomes at the &#34;tails&#34; of the distribution, including a clever &#34;humorous Oscar joke&#34; as an unlikely but plausible response.
- **Cultural Grounding**: Exp 3 showed that the model accurately shifts its behavior manifold based on cultural constraints (e.g., &#34;wa&#34; or harmony for the Japanese persona vs. &#34;disruptive honesty&#34; for the NYC persona).

## 5. Analysis &amp; Discussion
### Toward a Theory of Simulation
Our results support a **Manifold-based Theory of Simulation**:
1.  **The LLM as a Superset**: The pre-trained LLM represents a high-dimensional manifold of all human-documented behavior.
2.  **Conditioning as Projection**: Personas and scenarios act as constraints that project the probability mass onto specific sub-regions of this manifold.
3.  **The Alignment Bias**: Current SOTA models have a strong &#34;central tendency&#34; toward collaborative and safe behavior (the &#34;Helpful, Harmless, Honest&#34; mode). This manifests as &#34;mode collapse&#34; in the Generic Human persona.
4.  **Capturing Underlying Factors**: The model does not just &#34;mimic&#34; text; it &#34;solves&#34; for behavior by integrating latent factors (ambition, culture, friendship) into its output, as evidenced by the significant shifts in factor scores across personas.

## 6. Limitations
- **Model Bias**: The results are specific to GPT-4o. Models with different alignment training might exhibit different manifolds.
- **Sample Size**: While 50 branches per persona provide a trend, larger scales (N &gt; 1000) would be needed to map the true &#34;tails&#34; of the distribution.
- **Scenario Specificity**: Results might vary in high-stakes versus low-stakes environments.

## 7. Conclusions &amp; Next Steps
LLMs are indeed capable of simulating a superset of human outcomes. They capture &#34;underlying factors&#34; of human behavior with enough fidelity to differentiate between complex social strategies and cultural norms. However, to use them as true &#34;multiverse generators,&#34; researchers must use techniques like high-temperature sampling or explicit &#34;tail prompting&#34; to overcome the alignment-induced mode collapse of the base model.

**Next Steps**:
- Investigate the &#34;black swan&#34; generation capability of LLMs in economic systems.
- Develop a metric for &#34;Simulation Fidelity&#34; that compares LLM outcome manifolds to real-world behavioral datasets.

# Research Plan: A Theory of Simulation in Large Language Models

## Motivation &amp; Novelty Assessment

### Why This Research Matters
Understanding LLMs as simulators rather than just &#34;stochastic parrots&#34; or &#34;next-token predictors&#34; is crucial for their application in social sciences, policy testing, and risk assessment. If LLMs can simulate a superset of human behavioral outcomes, they can be used to explore &#34;what-if&#34; scenarios and &#34;black swan&#34; events that are difficult or impossible to observe in real-world data but are theoretically possible within the constraints of human psychology.

### Gap in Existing Work
Existing research (e.g., *Generative Agents*, *Homo Silicus*) mostly focuses on the *accuracy* or *believability* of LLM simulations—how well they match the *most likely* human behavior. There is a significant gap in exploring the **full distribution** of simulated outcomes. Specifically, can LLMs simulate the &#34;tails&#34; of human behavior? Does their training on a vast &#34;superset&#34; of human text allow them to simulate a &#34;superset&#34; of outcomes that exceeds any single individual&#39;s behavior but remains within the bounds of collective human possibility?

### Our Novel Contribution
We propose to move from &#34;point-estimate&#34; simulation (most likely outcome) to &#34;distributional&#34; simulation (multiversal outcomes). We will test the hypothesis that LLMs, by capturing the underlying factors of human behavior, can simulate a superset of outcomes. We will specifically investigate the &#34;branching&#34; behavior of LLMs in controlled social scenarios.

### Experiment Justification
- **Experiment 1: Multiverse Branching Analysis**: Why needed? To empirically demonstrate that LLMs maintain a &#34;superposition&#34; of possible futures and to characterize the diversity of these outcomes.
- **Experiment 2: Latent Factor Perturbation**: Why needed? To verify that the simulated outcomes are indeed driven by &#34;underlying factors&#34; (e.g., personality traits, situational constraints) rather than random noise.
- **Experiment 3: Plausibility of the &#34;Superset&#34;**: Why needed? To test if the generated outcomes, especially the &#34;tails&#34;, are perceived as human-plausible, even if they are not common in the training data.

## Research Question
What would a theory of simulation look like in the context of LLMs? Specifically, can LLMs simulate a superset of possible outcomes of human behavior by capturing its underlying factors?

## Hypothesis Decomposition
- **H1 (Multiversal Diversity)**: Given the same starting state, LLMs will generate a diverse set of plausible outcomes when sampled with non-zero temperature.
- **H2 (Factor Grounding)**: Systematic changes to the latent factors (persona traits) will lead to predictable shifts in the distribution of outcomes.
- **H3 (Superset Plausibility)**: Outcomes generated by LLMs include &#34;low-probability but high-plausibility&#34; events that are not frequent in typical human datasets but are consistent with human behavior.

## Proposed Methodology

### Approach
We will use a &#34;Multiverse Branching&#34; framework. We will take a set of &#34;starting states&#34; (scenarios + personas) and roll out multiple parallel continuations. We will then analyze the resulting distribution of behaviors.

### Experimental Steps
1. **Scenario Selection**: Extract scenarios from `reddit_confessions` and `PersonaHub`.
2. **Multiverse Generation**: For each scenario, generate $N$ parallel outcomes using a high-performing LLM (e.g., Claude 3.5 Sonnet or GPT-4o) with temperature &gt; 0.
3. **Feature Extraction**: Use a secondary LLM as an evaluator/coder to extract behavioral features (e.g., &#34;aggression level&#34;, &#34;empathy&#34;, &#34;decision outcome&#34;) from the generated text.
4. **Distributional Analysis**: Map the outcomes into a behavioral space and analyze the variance, entropy, and &#34;tail&#34; behavior.
5. **Factor Perturbation**: Vary persona traits (e.g., change &#34;Introvert&#34; to &#34;Extrovert&#34;) and repeat steps 2-4 to see how the &#34;outcome manifold&#34; shifts.

### Baselines
- **Human Baseline**: Where available, compare against real-world outcome distributions (e.g., social science data or original Reddit threads).
- **Random Baseline**: Compare LLM branching against &#34;random word&#34; or &#34;generic persona&#34; branching.

### Evaluation Metrics
- **Behavioral Entropy**: Diversity of outcomes.
- **Plausibility Score**: Evaluated by a separate LLM or human-in-the-loop.
- **Factor Sensitivity**: Correlation between perturbed factors and outcome features.

### Statistical Analysis Plan
- K-S tests to compare distributions between perturbed and control groups.
- Clustering analysis to identify &#34;basins&#34; of outcomes.
- Semantic similarity (BERTScore/Embeddings) to quantify outcome spread.

## Expected Outcomes
We expect to find that LLMs do indeed simulate a &#34;superset&#34; of outcomes. We predict that while most outcomes cluster around &#34;likely&#34; behavior, there will be a significant and plausible &#34;long tail&#34; of outcomes that demonstrate the model&#39;s understanding of complex behavioral factors.

# Literature Review: A Theory of Simulation in Large Language Models

## Research Area Overview
The field of &#34;simulation&#34; using Large Language Models (LLMs) has evolved from simple text generation to the conceptualization of LLMs as **multiverse generators** and **role-players**. This research area explores whether LLMs can faithfully simulate human behavior and whether they can generate a &#34;superset&#34; of possible outcomes based on underlying psychological and sociological factors.

## Key Papers

### 1. Multiversal views on language models (2021)
- **Authors**: Laria Reynolds, Kyle McDonell
- **Source**: arXiv: 2102.06391
- **Key Contribution**: Conceptualizes LLMs as &#34;multiverse generators&#34; rather than linear text predictors.
- **Methodology**: Introduces &#34;interpretational multiplicity&#34; (uncertainty of the present) and &#34;dynamic multiplicity&#34; (uncertainty of the future).
- **Relevance**: Directly addresses the research hypothesis about simulating a superset of possible outcomes.

### 2. Role-Play with Large Language Models (2023)
- **Authors**: Murray Shanahan, Kyle McDonell, Laria Reynolds
- **Source**: arXiv: 2305.16367
- **Key Contribution**: Argues that LLMs are &#34;simulacra&#34; in a &#34;superposition&#34; of possible characters.
- **Methodology**: Uses the metaphor of role-play to explain how LLMs exhibit complex behaviors without needing to be &#34;true&#34; agents.
- **Relevance**: Provides the theoretical bridge between probability distributions and simulated agency.

### 3. Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior (2023)
- **Authors**: Joon Sung Park, et al.
- **Source**: arXiv: 2304.03442
- **Key Contribution**: Demonstrated a society of agents (&#34;Smallville&#34;) that exhibits emergent social behavior.
- **Methodology**: Architecture involving a &#34;Memory Stream&#34;, &#34;Reflection&#34;, and &#34;Planning&#34;.
- **Relevance**: Practical implementation of simulation theory at scale.

### 4. Large Language Models as Simulated Economic Agents: Homo Silicus? (2023)
- **Authors**: John J. Horton
- **Source**: arXiv: 2303.07064
- **Key Contribution**: Explores LLMs as computational models of humans for social science.
- **Methodology**: Running classic economic experiments on LLM &#34;personas&#34;.
- **Relevance**: Shows how LLMs capture &#34;underlying factors&#34; of human behavior (economic rationality, biases).

### 5. AgentSociety: Large-Scale Simulation of LLM-Driven Generative Agents (2025)
- **Authors**: Piao, Jinghua, et al.
- **Source**: arXiv: 2502.08691
- **Key Contribution**: A framework for large-scale (city-level) simulation of generative agents.
- **Relevance**: Represents the state-of-the-art in urban and social simulation using LLMs.

## Common Methodologies
- **Role-Playing/Persona Prompting**: Assigning specific &#34;identities&#34; to LLMs to control behavior.
- **Memory Architectures**: Using vector databases or &#34;memory streams&#34; to maintain consistency.
- **Multiverse Branching**: Sampling multiple continuations from a single prompt to explore different outcomes.
- **Reflection &amp; Planning**: Higher-order cognitive loops where agents &#34;think&#34; about their own past behavior.

## Evaluation Metrics
- **Believability**: Human-centered evaluation of how &#34;real&#34; the simulation feels.
- **Consistency**: Maintaining the persona and history over time.
- **Alignment**: How closely the simulated behavior matches real-world human data (e.g., economic experiments).

## Gaps and Opportunities
- **Superset of Outcomes**: Most current work focuses on &#34;believable&#34; (likely) outcomes. There is a gap in exploring the &#34;tails&#34; of the distribution—what are the *possible but unlikely* outcomes that an LLM can simulate?
- **Theory of Simulation**: A formal mathematical theory of what it means for a probability distribution to be a &#34;valid&#34; simulator of a complex system (like human society).

## Recommendations for Our Experiment
- **Dataset**: Use `PersonaHub` to generate a diverse range of starting conditions.
- **Baseline**: Use the `Homo Silicus` framework to test if LLMs capture specific behavioral factors.
- **Experiment Design**: Implement a &#34;Multiverse&#34; branching experiment where the same initial scenario is rolled out 100+ times to observe the &#34;superset of outcomes&#34;.
