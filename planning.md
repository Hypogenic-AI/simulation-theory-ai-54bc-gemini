# Research Plan: A Theory of Simulation in Large Language Models

## Motivation & Novelty Assessment

### Why This Research Matters
Understanding LLMs as simulators rather than just "stochastic parrots" or "next-token predictors" is crucial for their application in social sciences, policy testing, and risk assessment. If LLMs can simulate a superset of human behavioral outcomes, they can be used to explore "what-if" scenarios and "black swan" events that are difficult or impossible to observe in real-world data but are theoretically possible within the constraints of human psychology.

### Gap in Existing Work
Existing research (e.g., *Generative Agents*, *Homo Silicus*) mostly focuses on the *accuracy* or *believability* of LLM simulations—how well they match the *most likely* human behavior. There is a significant gap in exploring the **full distribution** of simulated outcomes. Specifically, can LLMs simulate the "tails" of human behavior? Does their training on a vast "superset" of human text allow them to simulate a "superset" of outcomes that exceeds any single individual's behavior but remains within the bounds of collective human possibility?

### Our Novel Contribution
We propose to move from "point-estimate" simulation (most likely outcome) to "distributional" simulation (multiversal outcomes). We will test the hypothesis that LLMs, by capturing the underlying factors of human behavior, can simulate a superset of outcomes. We will specifically investigate the "branching" behavior of LLMs in controlled social scenarios.

### Experiment Justification
- **Experiment 1: Multiverse Branching Analysis**: Why needed? To empirically demonstrate that LLMs maintain a "superposition" of possible futures and to characterize the diversity of these outcomes.
- **Experiment 2: Latent Factor Perturbation**: Why needed? To verify that the simulated outcomes are indeed driven by "underlying factors" (e.g., personality traits, situational constraints) rather than random noise.
- **Experiment 3: Plausibility of the "Superset"**: Why needed? To test if the generated outcomes, especially the "tails", are perceived as human-plausible, even if they are not common in the training data.

## Research Question
What would a theory of simulation look like in the context of LLMs? Specifically, can LLMs simulate a superset of possible outcomes of human behavior by capturing its underlying factors?

## Hypothesis Decomposition
- **H1 (Multiversal Diversity)**: Given the same starting state, LLMs will generate a diverse set of plausible outcomes when sampled with non-zero temperature.
- **H2 (Factor Grounding)**: Systematic changes to the latent factors (persona traits) will lead to predictable shifts in the distribution of outcomes.
- **H3 (Superset Plausibility)**: Outcomes generated by LLMs include "low-probability but high-plausibility" events that are not frequent in typical human datasets but are consistent with human behavior.

## Proposed Methodology

### Approach
We will use a "Multiverse Branching" framework. We will take a set of "starting states" (scenarios + personas) and roll out multiple parallel continuations. We will then analyze the resulting distribution of behaviors.

### Experimental Steps
1. **Scenario Selection**: Extract scenarios from `reddit_confessions` and `PersonaHub`.
2. **Multiverse Generation**: For each scenario, generate $N$ parallel outcomes using a high-performing LLM (e.g., Claude 3.5 Sonnet or GPT-4o) with temperature > 0.
3. **Feature Extraction**: Use a secondary LLM as an evaluator/coder to extract behavioral features (e.g., "aggression level", "empathy", "decision outcome") from the generated text.
4. **Distributional Analysis**: Map the outcomes into a behavioral space and analyze the variance, entropy, and "tail" behavior.
5. **Factor Perturbation**: Vary persona traits (e.g., change "Introvert" to "Extrovert") and repeat steps 2-4 to see how the "outcome manifold" shifts.

### Baselines
- **Human Baseline**: Where available, compare against real-world outcome distributions (e.g., social science data or original Reddit threads).
- **Random Baseline**: Compare LLM branching against "random word" or "generic persona" branching.

### Evaluation Metrics
- **Behavioral Entropy**: Diversity of outcomes.
- **Plausibility Score**: Evaluated by a separate LLM or human-in-the-loop.
- **Factor Sensitivity**: Correlation between perturbed factors and outcome features.

### Statistical Analysis Plan
- K-S tests to compare distributions between perturbed and control groups.
- Clustering analysis to identify "basins" of outcomes.
- Semantic similarity (BERTScore/Embeddings) to quantify outcome spread.

## Expected Outcomes
We expect to find that LLMs do indeed simulate a "superset" of outcomes. We predict that while most outcomes cluster around "likely" behavior, there will be a significant and plausible "long tail" of outcomes that demonstrate the model's understanding of complex behavioral factors.

## Potential Challenges
- **API Costs**: High $N$ for branching might be expensive. (Mitigation: use $N=20-50$ instead of $100+$).
- **Evaluation Bias**: Using an LLM to evaluate an LLM might introduce bias. (Mitigation: use clear rubrics and multiple evaluation models).

## Success Criteria
- Successful generation and categorization of multiversal outcomes.
- Statistically significant shift in outcome distribution upon factor perturbation.
- Demonstration of "low-probability, high-plausibility" outcomes in the tails.
